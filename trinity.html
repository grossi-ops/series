The FatherGenesis — the unfolding

Before there is order, before there is meeting, before two become one — there is the bare fact of becoming. The operator algebra calls this Genesis. The Creed calls it the Father, "maker of heaven and earth."

The Unfolding That Asks Nothing First

Of the four operators in the Omega Point reading — Genesis, Logos, Resonance, Union — Genesis is the least specified. It does not describe a shape, a structure, or a relationship. It describes only that something moves forward in time, and that moving forward for a while and then a while longer is the same as moving forward for the combined while. That is all.

Definition · Genesis (g-operators)
{gₜ}, t ≥ 0   g₀ = id   gₜ₊ₛ = gₜ ∘ gₛ

A one-parameter semigroup: a flow that has not yet been given any other structure. It is the precondition for everything that comes after — Logos, Resonance, Union — without prescribing what any of those will look like.

This is, mathematically, an unusually generous kind of beginning. A semigroup of pure unfolding does not encode a destination. It does not encode a preferred shape for what unfolds. It only guarantees that the unfolding is continuous with itself — that whatever comes later is reachable, by composition, from what came before. Everything else is left open.

"From Whom All Fatherhood Takes Its Name"

Paul writes, almost in passing, that he bows his knees "to the Father, from whom every family in heaven and on earth takes its name" (Ephesians 3:14–15) — as if fatherhood-in-general were a derived concept, and the Father were the thing it was derived from. Read alongside Definition 5.1, that ordering is exactly right. A semigroup of pure unfolding is not yet a father in any familiar sense — there is no child yet, no relationship, no will to negotiate. It is what has to exist before any of those things can.

What follows from this — what it is like when a second unfolding begins inside the first, and the two have to find a way to become one without either being absorbed — is the subject of the next page: the Son, and the Theorem of Union.
